One Product Is Not a Plan: The Stewardship Order Christian Families Miss

The families I sit with usually own five financial products and no plan.

A 401(k) at work. A whole life policy a man at church sold them in their twenties. A Roth IRA at Fidelity. A 529 for the kids that grandparents started. A checking account at a bank the family no longer likes. Every product is a snapshot of who was selling what the week it got opened. None of them is a plan.

A biblical stewardship plan is not a product. A stewardship plan is the order money moves through your life. Income comes in. Margin gets built. Giving gets funded. Investments fill. An estate gets prepared. In that order, every month, on purpose. When the order is right, the products serve the plan. When the order is wrong, the products run the family.

A product is not a plan

A product is a transaction. A plan is a discipline. The reason a family can feel anxious about money even when the numbers on paper look fine is that they own products and live without order. The 401(k) auto-deducts on the 15th. The whole life premium auto-pays on the 1st. The 529 quietly underfunds itself while the family wonders why generosity feels squeezed.

Owning five products without an order is like owning five appliances without a kitchen. You can technically cook, but you spend half your time finding the cord.

Why secular planners miss the theology behind your money

A secular planner will look at your products and rearrange them. The 401(k) gets a different allocation. The whole life gets reviewed for a 1035 exchange. The Roth gets balanced against the 529. The whole conversation happens at the product layer.

What does not happen is the layer underneath. The doctrine of providence. The theology of margin. The biblical posture toward giving. The question of what wealth is even for. A secular planner cannot help you answer those questions because the categories are not in his toolkit.

Plain Reformed teaching on money starts at the doctrine, not at the spreadsheet. God owns the cattle on a thousand hills, which means you are the steward of a portfolio you did not create. Your job is not maximization. Your job is faithfulness with what was entrusted.

That changes how you sequence everything below it.

The biblical order of money

Stewardship has an order. It is not "give whatever feels right and then save whatever is left." It is a sequence the Scripture and historical pastoral practice both attest:

  1. Income. What comes in is the input.
  2. Giving. First fruits, not leftovers. The tithe (or the proportional gift, by conviction) is taken from the top, not from the bottom.
  3. Margin. A buffer above expenses. This is the cash that keeps small troubles from becoming large ones. Three to six months of household burn is the common target, more if the income is variable.
  4. Investing. Long-horizon ownership. Retirement, education funding, business equity. Money put to work that you do not need this year.
  5. Estate. What gets passed to the next generation, and what gets given on your way out.

Families often run the order backward without knowing it. They invest first because the 401(k) auto-deducts. They give second because the church's giving link is easy. They build margin third, if at all, because nobody told them margin was a category. They handle estate last because thinking about death is hard.

The order matters because the order is the plan.

A pastoral example: the Mitchells in Colorado Springs

Consider a family I worked with. Three kids, both spouses in their late thirties, the husband running a small contracting business with about $340,000 in personal income, the wife homeschooling. By every external measure they were doing well. Internally they felt squeezed.

The product map looked solid: a SEP-IRA with $84,000, a 529 with $11,000 split across three children, a whole life policy on the husband, a term policy on the wife, two checking accounts, one savings account at $4,200. Giving was running at about 6% of gross.

The plan, when we built it, looked nothing like the product map. We held the giving at 10% from the top. We rebuilt margin to $32,000, which was about four months of household burn. We paused the 529 contributions for six months while the margin filled. We rewrote the contracting business' draw schedule so the husband paid himself on the 1st and the 15th instead of whenever cash felt thick. Then, with the order set, the 529 resumed and the SEP-IRA grew without the family feeling cornered.

The products did not change much. The order changed everything. The husband told his wife six months later he felt like he could breathe at the dinner table again. That was not a product breakthrough. That was a stewardship plan finally running in the right sequence.

What a stewardship plan looks like in practice

Three traits make a stewardship plan a plan rather than a wishlist.

First, it is ordered. Income, giving, margin, investing, estate. Every dollar has a place it goes before the family decides emotionally. The order is the plan.

Second, it is named. Each category has a target number. Giving is a percentage, not a feeling. Margin is dollars, not a vague comfort level. Investing has a contribution rate, not a "we will get to it." Naming the numbers takes the conversation out of the realm of anxiety and into the realm of stewardship.

Third, it is reviewable. A plan that nobody reads is not a plan. A stewardship plan gets revisited at least annually, with the spouses, and the order gets verified. Income changes. Margin gets used and rebuilt. Investing rates step up over time. The plan is alive.
